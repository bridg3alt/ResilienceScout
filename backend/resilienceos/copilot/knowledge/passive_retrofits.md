# Passive Retrofits for Heat Resilience

## Cool roofs
Flat RCC roofs on Indian public buildings absorb 80-90% of incident solar
radiation, driving up top-floor temperatures. A high-reflectance (cool-roof)
coating cuts solar absorptance to roughly 20-30%, lowering peak indoor
temperature on the top floor and reducing cooling load. Cool roofs are among the
lowest-cost, highest-return resilience measures and are promoted under the India
Cooling Action Plan.

## Glazing and shading
Single clear glazing has a high solar heat gain coefficient (SHGC ~0.8), letting
most solar energy into the space. Tinting, low-e double glazing (SHGC ~0.4), or
external shading (chajjas, louvres, vegetation) substantially reduce solar gains
and peak indoor temperature.

## Thermal mass and night ventilation
Heavy masonry/concrete construction has high thermal mass, which damps and delays
indoor temperature swings. This mass is only beneficial if it is discharged
overnight by ventilation; otherwise heat accumulates day over day during a
prolonged heatwave.

## Prioritisation
For heat survivability (as opposed to backup power), envelope measures — cool
roof, glazing, shading, ventilation — move indoor temperature the most. Solar PV
and batteries improve backup duration for critical loads but do not by themselves
make a space cooler when the grid is down.
